Benton County’s operating model treats the Cost Approach not as a static national table, but as a market-calibrated measurement system: verified sales inputs → locally calibrated percent-of-base curves and cost tables → holdout tests and vertical-equity diagnostics (PRD/PRB) → governed change control with audit trails and peer sign-off. 1. Introduction: why assessment is central to tax fairness (even when it isn’t the whole story)
Washington’s fairness debate often begins and ends with the claim “property taxes are regressive.” That statement is frequently used as a proxy for broader concerns: rising housing costs, wage stagnation, and a state tax mix that relies heavily on consumption. Those are real issues. But they are analytically distinct from assessment equity—and if we do not separate them, policy solutions become confused and credibility suffers.

Assessment administration cannot redesign the state tax mix. It cannot set levy limits, approve bonds, or decide the share of revenue raised through sales versus income taxation. What assessors can do—critically—is prevent the property tax base from being allocated unfairly across value tiers. That is not a minor technicality. If tier bias is present, it functions as a quiet redistribution inside the property tax: a structural advantage for higher-value properties and a structural disadvantage for lower-value properties. Even if the tax mix remains unchanged, eliminating tier bias is a direct fairness gain.

Benton County’s approach frames assessment as an evidence-tested measurement system with governance, comparable to other high-stakes public measurement regimes (auditing, public health surveillance, traffic safety reporting). This paper explains that system, the diagnostic metrics used to detect regressivity inside the base, and the practical steps Benton uses to prevent sales chasing while improving vertical equity.

2. Definitions: three questions that must not be conflated
2.1 Housing affordability
Driven by supply constraints, construction costs, financing, wage growth, and demand. Assessment does not create or solve supply.

2.2 Tax incidence (regressivity/progressivity across incomes)
Incidence depends on who ultimately bears the burden as a share of income. In Washington, regressivity debates typically focus on the overall state/local tax structure. Assessors do not control that structure.

2.3 Assessment equity (proportionality inside the property tax base)
Assessment equity asks: Are properties assessed proportionally to market value across the value spectrum? If not, the property tax can become more regressive (or distortive) than it already would be under a uniform base.

Key point:
Even when broader tax incidence remains debated, assessment equity is a prerequisite for legitimacy. It is the difference between “people dislike taxes” and “the base is structurally unfair.”

3. The mechanism: how “hidden regressivity” can arise inside property tax
Tier bias occurs when assessment ratios vary systematically with value—most commonly when ratios decline as market value increases. This can happen for many reasons: model misspecification, outdated costing parameters, uncorrected quality-grade drift, neighborhood stratification gaps, or operational shortcuts. The result is predictable:

High-value properties carry a smaller share of the tax base than intended.

Lower-value properties carry a larger share than intended.

During rate spikes (often levy-driven), these distortions become highly visible in dollars, intensifying perception and appeals.

3.1 Measuring tier bias
Benton treats vertical equity as a required output, using standard diagnostics such as:

PRD (Price-Related Differential): whether ratios drift with price (near 1.00 indicates neutrality)

PRB (Price-Related Bias): slope-based bias metric (near 0 indicates neutrality)

Ratio-by-tier plots: quartiles/deciles with confidence bands and stability checks

Holdout reporting: metrics on holdout samples to avoid “overfitting the sold set”

Benton’s published internal performance summary reports PRD ≈ 0.99 (near value-neutral) alongside strong level and uniformity.

4. The Benton Method: market-calibrated costing as a measurement system
Benton’s core idea is operationally simple but technically rigorous:

Treat the Cost Approach as a market-calibrated measurement system, not a static national table.
Replacement cost new (RCN) parameters are calibrated from verified sales evidence, expressed through percent-of-base curves and local cost tables, then tested for both level and vertical equity and governed through version control and peer review.

4.1 System architecture (Inputs → Calibration → QA → Governance)
Inputs

Verified arms-length sales file (qualified sales)

Property characteristics and stratification variables (neighborhood, quality, condition, size, age, etc.)

Calibration

Percent-of-Base curves and local cost tables calibrated to local evidence

Adjustments applied through standardized tables/curves (not ad hoc parcel edits)

Quality assurance

Holdout tests

Governance

Change control with audit trails

Versioned tables and calibration memos for each cycle

Peer review / independent review sign-off

This “measurement stack” matters because regressivity in the base is a measurement failure. The defense is not persuasion; it is diagnostics and governance.

5. Preventing sales chasing while improving equity
A common critique of any calibration regime is “sales chasing”—the improper practice of disproportionately adjusting sold properties (or their close comparables) to match sale prices, producing artificially strong ratio statistics on the sold set without improving the unsold population.

Benton’s controls target the known failure modes:

5.1 Failure mode: overfitting the sold set
Control: maintain a holdout sample and report performance on holdout and stability proxies, not only in-sample sold ratios.

5.2 Failure mode: manual tuning to “hit” one sale
Control: model-first, sale-second—apply changes via tables/curves (systemic), keep parcel-level exceptions rare, logged, and reviewable.

5.3 Failure mode: strata drift (tier bias)
Control: require PRD/PRB by price tier and compare before/after; neutrality must be demonstrated or a documented policy rationale is required.

5.4 Failure mode: unreviewed tweaks
Control: version tables, require peer review sign-off, and maintain a calibration memo for every cycle.

These controls ensure Benton’s calibration improves both performance and defensibility. The point for policymakers is straightforward: a modern assessment system can be simultaneously accurate, equitable, and auditable.

6. Tax bill reality: separating assessment from levy policy
A recurring taxpayer belief is: “If my assessed value goes up, you raised my taxes.” This is often false or incomplete because the property tax bill depends on both value and the levy stack.

Benton’s counter materials use a simple identity:

Tax Bill = Taxable Value × (Total Rate / 1,000)
Where “Total Rate” is the sum of levy lines applying to the parcel (state, county, city, schools, special purpose, voted debt).

6.1 Year-over-year decomposition (counter-proof)
Benton decomposes the change in tax into:

ΔTax = (ΔValue × prior-year rate) + (prior-year value × ΔRate) + (ΔValue × ΔRate)

This yields three explainable components:

Value effect: “Your share of the base changed”

Rate/levy effect: “The levy stack changed due to budgets/limits/votes/state policy”

Interaction: usually small, included for completeness

6.2 Why this matters for regressivity
When levy rates spike, dollar impacts amplify. If tier bias exists (e.g., high-value ratios drift low), the system’s inequity becomes more visible and politically destabilizing. A value-neutral base is therefore a stabilizer: it ensures rate/levy changes (policy choices) are not compounded by hidden structural bias in the base.
